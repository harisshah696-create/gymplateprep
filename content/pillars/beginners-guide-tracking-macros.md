# How to Track Macros: A Beginner's Guide to Counting Macros

Tracking macros — short for macronutrients (protein, carbohydrates, and fat) — is the most reliable way to control your body composition.

It removes guesswork. Instead of asking "am I eating too much?" you look at the numbers. Instead of hoping you ate enough protein, you know.

This guide covers the complete system: **how to calculate your numbers, track your food, and stay consistent without losing your mind.**

---

# 1. What Are Macros?

Macronutrients are the three nutrients your body needs in large amounts:

| Macro | Calories Per Gram | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| **Protein** | 4 kcal | Muscle repair, recovery, satiety |
| **Carbohydrates** | 4 kcal | Energy for training and daily activity |
| **Fat** | 9 kcal | Hormone production, health, vitamin absorption |

**Calories are the foundation. Macros are the optimization.**

You can lose weight eating 2,000 calories of donuts (see: the Twinkie diet experiment), but you'll lose muscle, feel terrible, and struggle with hunger. The right macro split keeps you full, energized, and preserves muscle.

# 2. Calculate Your Macros

Track your current intake for 3–5 days first (using an app like MyFitnessPal or MacroFactor). This gives you a baseline.

Then use the formulas below to set targets.

---

## Step 1: Find Your Maintenance Calories

**Maintenance calories** = the calories you need to stay at your current weight.

### Quick Estimate

| Activity Level | Multiplier × Body Weight (lbs) |
| --- | ---: |
| Sedentary (desk job, no exercise) | 12–13 |
| Light Activity (1–3 workouts/week) | 13–14 |
| Moderate (3–5 workouts/week) | 14–16 |
| Very Active (6–7 workouts/week) | 16–18 |

**Example:** 175 lb male, moderate activity
175 × 15 = **2,625 kcal (estimated maintenance)**

### More Precise Formula (Mifflin-St Jeor)

| Gender | Formula |
| --- | --- |
| **Male** | 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) — 5 × age(y) + 5 |
| **Female** | 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) — 5 × age(y) — 161 |

Then multiply by activity factor (1.2–1.9) to get maintenance.

---

## Step 2: Set Your Goal Calories

| Goal | Adjustment |
| --- | --- |
| **Muscle Gain** | Maintenance + 300–500 kcal |
| **Weight Loss** | Maintenance — 300–500 kcal |
| **Recomposition (recomp)** | Maintenance (eat at maintenance, train hard) |

---

## Step 3: Set Your Macro Split

### Muscle Gain (Bulking)

| Macro | % Calories | For 3,000 kcal |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| Protein | 25–30% | 188–225 g |
| Carbohydrates | 45–55% | 338–413 g |
| Fat | 20–25% | 67–83 g |

### Weight Loss (Cutting)

| Macro | % Calories | For 2,200 kcal |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| Protein | 35–40% | 193–220 g |
| Carbohydrates | 30–35% | 165–193 g |
| Fat | 25–30% | 61–73 g |

### Simplified Setup (Works for Most People)

| Macro | Formula |
| --- | --- |
| **Protein** | 1.6–2.2 g per kg body weight (same for bulking AND cutting) |
| **Fat** | 0.5–1.0 g per kg body weight (minimum) |
| **Carbs** | Remaining calories |

**This simplified method prioritizes protein (the most important macro) and fills the rest with carbs and fat based on preference.**

# 4. How to Track: Step by Step

## Step 1 — Weigh Your Food

Always weigh food **raw/uncooked** when possible. Cooking changes water weight and can skew calorie counts by 20–30%.

| Food | Weigh Raw | Weigh Cooked |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chicken breast | ✅ 100 g raw = ~31 g protein | ❌ 100 g cooked = ~38 g protein (water loss) |
| Rice | ✅ 100 g dry = ~350 kcal | ❌ 100 g cooked = ~130 kcal |
| Oats | ✅ 50 g dry | ❌ Same as cooked (oats don't change much) |
| Vegetables | ❌ Minimal difference | ✅ Fine either way |
| Meat with bone | ✅ Weigh after cooking, subtract bone | ❌ |

**Pro tip:** Cook in batches, weigh raw before cooking, then divide total weight into portions.

## Step 2 — Log Everything

This includes:

* Cooking oils (120 kcal per tablespoon)
* Sauces, dressings, marinades
* Nut butters (100 kcal per tablespoon)
* Beverages with calories (milk, juice, protein shakes)
* "Just one bite" (it adds up)

### What About Cheat Meals?

Don't log them perfectly — estimate. A restaurant burger is ~700–900 kcal. A slice of pizza is ~300–400 kcal. Overestimating beats underestimating.

## Step 3 — Review and Adjust

Weigh yourself **daily** (same time, same conditions — morning, after bathroom, before food/drink).

Look at the **weekly average** (not daily fluctuations). Water weight, sodium, and glycogen changes cause daily swings of 1–4 lbs.

**Adjust based on 2-week trends:**

| Goal | Weight Change | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Muscle Gain | Gaining 0.25–0.5% body weight/week | ✅ On track |
| Muscle Gain | Gaining less | +100–200 kcal |
| Muscle Gain | Gaining more than 0.5%/week | −100–150 kcal |
| Weight Loss | Losing 0.5–1% body weight/week | ✅ On track |
| Weight Loss | Losing less | −100–200 kcal |
| Weight Loss | Losing more than 1%/week | +100–200 kcal |

# 5. Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Not Weighing Food

"Eyeballing" turns a 500-calorie meal into 800 calories faster than you think.

**Fix:** Use a kitchen scale for the first 2–4 weeks. It takes 10 seconds per meal and trains your eye.

---

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Cooking Oils and Sauces

A "healthy" salad becomes 700 calories with 3 tablespoons of olive oil dressing.

| "Healthy" Ingredient | Calories |
| --- | ---: |
| Olive oil (1 tbsp) | 120 |
| Peanut butter (2 tbsp) | 190 |
| Honey (1 tbsp) | 64 |
| Balsamic vinaigrette (2 tbsp) | 100 |

**Fix:** Measure oils and sauces with measuring spoons. Always.

---

### Mistake 3: Trusting Restaurant Nutrition Info

Restaurant calorie counts can be off by 50–200 calories per item.

**Fix:** Add 20–30% to listed restaurant calories. Always overestimate.

---

### Mistake 4: Quitting After One Bad Day

A single bad day won't matter. A week of bad days won't even matter much.

**What matters:** Consistent adherence over months and years.

**Fix:** Log the bad day honestly. Move on tomorrow. One day at maintenance or slightly over doesn't undo a week of adherence.

---

### Mistake 5: Obsessing Over Perfection

Trying to hit macros within 1 gram is unnecessary and stressful.

**Good enough:** Protein within 5 g, carbs within 15 g, fat within 5 g, calories within 100 kcal.

# 7. Flexible Dieting vs Strict Tracking

## Flexible Dieting (IIFYM)

Eat whatever fits your macros. If you want a donut and it fits, you eat the donut.

**Pros:** No foods are off-limits. Sustainable long-term.
**Cons:** Easy to overeat calorie-dense foods. "Donuts, protein powder, and rice cakes" is technically on target but nutritionally poor.

## Strict Tracking

Eat whole foods, meal prep, minimal processed food.

**Pros:** Better micronutrients, more satiety, healthier relationship with food.
**Cons:** Less flexible, harder to sustain socially.

## The Middle Ground (Recommended)

Aim for **80% whole foods, 20% flexible.** Meal prep your lunches and most dinners. Keep some flexibility for social eating and occasional treats.

This balance is sustainable and gives you 90%+ of the results.

# Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do I need to track macros?

**Minimum 2–4 weeks.** This teaches you proper portions and food composition.

Most people who track for 4–6 weeks can maintain their weight by eye afterward. However, for active goal pursuit (cutting or bulking), tracking throughout the process gives the best results.

---

### Do I need to track vegetables?

Leafy greens and non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, spinach, zucchini) have negligible calories — roughly 25–50 kcal per serving.

**Practical approach:** Don't track them unless you're eating very large quantities. They're essentially free calories that improve health and satiety.

---

### What about fiber?

Fiber is a carbohydrate but isn't digested. In most countries, **net carbs** (total carbs — fiber) are what matter.

In the US, food labels already subtract fiber from total carbs on some labels. In Cronometer and MacroFactor, fiber is shown separately.

**Practical approach:** Don't stress about fiber in your carb target. Aim for 25–35 g of fiber daily for health, but it doesn't affect your macro math.

---

### How accurate do food labels need to be?

Packaged food labels can legally be off by **20%** in either direction. Fresh foods (meat, produce) vary naturally.

**Don't worry about this.** The variation averages out over days and weeks. Consistent tracking with the same database creates a reliable baseline you can adjust from.

---

### Can I track macros as a vegetarian?

Yes. Protein is harder but achievable:

* Greek yogurt (20 g per cup)
* Cottage cheese (14 g per 1/2 cup)
* Tofu (20 g per 1/2 block)
* Seitan (21 g per 3 oz)
* Tempeh (15 g per 1/2 cup)
* Protein powder (25 g per scoop)
* Lentils (9 g per 1/2 cup cooked)
* Edamame (9 g per 1/2 cup)

Aim for 1.8–2.2 g/kg protein and include a variety of sources.

---

### Should I track alcohol?

Alcohol has ~7 kcal per gram but is metabolized differently than macros. It also reduces fat oxidation while it's being processed.

**Track it.** Most tracking apps have entries for beer, wine, and liquor. Add them as calories (not as carbs or fat) if the app allows custom entries.

---

### Do macros matter as much as calories?

For **weight change**, calories matter most. The Twinkie diet proved this.

For **body composition** (losing fat vs muscle, gaining muscle vs fat), macros matter significantly. Protein is the most important variable after total calories.

---

# Key Takeaways

* **Protein is the priority** — 1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight, every day, regardless of goal
* **Weigh your food** — a $15 kitchen scale is the best investment you can make
* **Track for 2–4 weeks minimum** — it trains your eye for lifelong portion awareness
* **Don't trust your eyeball** — oil, sauce, and nut butter calories add up fast
* **Log honestly** — a bad day logged is data; a bad day ignored is denial
* **Adjust based on 2-week trends** — daily weight fluctuates, weekly averages don't lie
* **80% whole foods, 20% flexible** — sustainable tracking beats perfect tracking every time
